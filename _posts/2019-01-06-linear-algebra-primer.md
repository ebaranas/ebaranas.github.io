<h4 id="non-zero-determinant">1. Non-zero determinant</h4>
<p>Equation <a href="eq.3">3</a> is the generalisation of the scalar equation <span class="katex--inline"><span class="katex"><span class="katex-mathml"><math><semantics><mrow><mi>x</mi><mo>=</mo><msup><mi>a</mi><mrow><mo>−</mo><mn>1</mn></mrow></msup><mi>y</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">x = a^{-1} y</annotation></semantics></math></span><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height: 0.43056em; vertical-align: 0em;"></span><span class="mord mathit">x</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right: 0.277778em;"></span><span class="mrel">=</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right: 0.277778em;"></span></span><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height: 1.00855em; vertical-align: -0.19444em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathit">a</span><span class="msupsub"><span class="vlist-t"><span class="vlist-r"><span class="vlist" style="height: 0.814108em;"><span class="" style="top: -3.063em; margin-right: 0.05em;"><span class="pstrut" style="height: 2.7em;"></span><span class="sizing reset-size6 size3 mtight"><span class="mord mtight"><span class="mord mtight">−</span><span class="mord mtight">1</span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span><span class="mord mathit" style="margin-right: 0.03588em;">y</span></span></span></span></span>. This is the reason why we do not want singular matrices: a matrix with dependent column vectors will always “force” other vectors to fit in its limited span <sup class="footnote-ref"><a href="#fn2" id="fnref2">2</a></sup> and it’s hard to recover the vector (invert) once it’s “squished”.</p>
<p><span class="katex--inline"><span class="katex"><span class="katex-mathml"><math><semantics><mrow><mi mathvariant="bold">A</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\mathbf A</annotation></semantics></math></span><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height: 0.68611em; vertical-align: 0em;"></span><span class="mord mathbf">A</span></span></span></span></span> is invertible if it is square and full rank. We will clarify what this means in the next section.</p>
<h2 id="solutions-to-linear-systems">Solutions to linear systems</h2>
<p>Equation <a href="#eq.3">3</a> is only valid if <span class="katex--inline"><span class="katex"><span class="katex-mathml"><math><semantics><mrow><mi mathvariant="bold">A</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\mathbf A</annotation></semantics></math></span><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height: 0.68611em; vertical-align: 0em;"></span><span class="mord mathbf">A</span></span></span></span></span> is a one-to-one transformation: every input corresponds to a unique output. However, in general, Eq. <a href="#eq.1">1</a> cannot be solved by inversion either because multiple inputs correspond to one output, or multiple outputs correspond to one input.</p>

<p><span class="katex--display"><span class="katex-display"><span class="katex"><span class="katex-mathml"><math><semantics><mrow><mi mathvariant="bold">A</mi><msub><mi mathvariant="bold">x</mi><mi mathvariant="script">N</mi></msub><mo>=</mo><mn>0.</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">

vertical-align: 0em;"></span><span class="mord mathit" style="margin-right: 0.02778em;">r</span></span></span></span></span>. An invertible matrix will have a full rank <span class="katex--inline"><span class="katex"><span class="katex-mathml"><math><semantics><mrow><mi>r</mi><mo>=</mo><mi>m</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">r = m</annotation></semantics></math></span><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height: 0.43056em; vertical-align: 0em;"></span><span class="mord mathit" style="margin-right: 0.02778em;">r</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right: 0.277778em;"></span><span class="mrel">=</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right: 0.277778em;"></span></span><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height: 0.43056em; vertical-align: 0em;"></span><span class="mord mathit">m</span></span></span></span></span>, which also means it must be square. The equivalent rank-nullity theorem is also true for row spaces because of Eq. <a href="#eq.8">8</a>.</em></p>
<p>Through the rank-nullity theorem, we learn two things:</p>
<ul>
<li>All invertible matrices are square because rectangular matrices will have at least one dependent vector either in the row or column space (whichever has the higher dimension). However, not all square matrices are invertible (happens when <span class="katex--inline"><span class="katex"><span class="katex-mathml"><math><semantics><mrow><mi>r</mi><mo>&amp;lt;</mo><mi>n</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">r &amp;lt; n</annotation></semantics></math></span><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height: 0.5782em; vertical-align: -0.0391em;"></span><span class="mord mathit" style="margin-right: 0.02778em;">r</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right: 0.277778em;"></span><span class="mrel">&lt;</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right: 0.277778em;"></span></span><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height: 0.43056em; vertical-align: 0em;"></span><span class="mord mathit">n</span></span></span></span></span>).</li>
<li>The bigger null space, the more “pathological” our system will be.</li>
</ul>
