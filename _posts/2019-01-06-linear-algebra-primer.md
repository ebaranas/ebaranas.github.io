<p>Recall that the columns (rows) of an <span class="katex--inline"><span class="katex"><span class="katex-mathml"><math><semantics><mrow><mi>n</mi><mo>×</mo><mi>m</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">n \times m</annotation></semantics></math></span><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height: 0.66666em; vertical-align: -0.08333em;"></span><span class="mord mathit">n</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right: 0.222222em;"></span><span class="mbin">×</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right: 0.222222em;"></span></span><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height: 0.43056em; vertical-align: 0em;"></span><span class="mord mathit">m</span></span></span></span></span> matrix can be visualised as vectors in an <span class="katex--inline"><span class="katex"><span class="katex-mathml"><math><semantics><mrow><mi>n</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">n</annotation></semantics></math></span><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height: 0.43056em; vertical-align: 0em;"></span><span class="mord mathit">n</span></span></span></span></span>-dim (<span class="katex--inline"><span class="katex"><span class="katex-mathml"><math><semantics><mrow><mi>m</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">m</annotation></semantics></math></span><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height: 0.43056em; vertical-align: 0em;"></span><span class="mord mathit">m</span></span></span></span></span>-dim) space. For this we need to define the <strong>rank</strong> of a matrix, which is the dimension of the space spanned by the column vectors of <span class="katex--inline"><span class="katex"><span class="katex-mathml"><math><semantics><mrow><mi mathvariant="bold">A</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\mathbf A</annotation></semantics></math></span><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height: 0.68611em; vertical-align: 0em;"></span><span class="mord mathbf">A</span></span></span></span></span>:</p>

<p>which is a reflection of the fact that the number of column (row) vectors is the sum of the number of independent and dependent vectors in the column (row) space.</p>
<p>Through the rank-nullity theorem, we learn two things:</p>
<ul>
<li>The bigger <span class="katex--inline"><span class="katex"><span class="katex-mathml"><math><semantics><mrow><mi>dim</mi><mo>⁡</mo><mi mathvariant="script">N</mi><mo>(</mo><mi mathvariant="bold">A</mi><mo>)</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\dim \mathcal N(\mathbf A)</annotation></semantics></math></span><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height: 1em; vertical-align: -0.25em;"></span><span class="mop">dim</span><span class="mspace" style="margin-right: 0.166667em;"></span><span class="mord mathcal" style="margin-right: 0.14736em;">N</span><span class="mopen">(</span><span class="mord mathbf">A</span><span class="mclose">)</span></span></span></span></span> is, the more “pathological” our system will be.</li>
<li>Invertible matrices are guaranteed to be square matrices, because a rectangular matrix will surely have a dependent vector either in the row or column space (whichever has the higher dimension), due to Eq. <a href="eq.9">9</a>. (However, not all square matrices will be invertible).</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="full-picture-solutions-to-linear-spaces">Full picture: solutions to linear spaces</h3>
<p>We are now ready to give the full picture of the size of the solution space given different shapes and types of <span class="katex--inline"><span class="katex"><span class="katex-mathml"><math><semantics><mrow><mi mathvariant="bold">A</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\mathbf A</annotation></semantics></math></span><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height: 0.68611em; vertical-align: 0em;"></span><span class="mord mathbf">A</span></span></span></span></span>.</p>
<p>Intuitively, we might think that overdetermined systems (thin <span class="katex--inline"><span class="katex"><span class="katex-mathml"><math><semantics><mrow><mi mathvariant="bold">A</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\mathbf A</annotation></semantics></math></span><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height: 0.68611em; vertical-align: 0em;"></span><span class="mord mathbf">A</span></span></span></span></span>)  have no solutions because there are more equations than unknowns, i.e., no single solution could simultaneously satisfy multiple equations.
